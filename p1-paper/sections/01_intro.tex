\section{Introduction}

Verified software is expected to evolve, but the artifacts that support verification often remain tightly coupled to the accidental structure of the program being verified. In ordinary software engineering, refactoring is a routine maintenance activity rather than an exceptional event~\cite{Mens2004RefactoringSurvey}, and empirical studies show that open-source projects perform a broad range of structural transformations as part of everyday evolution~\cite{Kim2012RefactoringInTheWild,Tsantalis2014RefactoringOperations}. More generally, maintenance-oriented software-engineering research has emphasized that software artifacts co-evolve over time and are best understood through revision-aware analysis rather than isolated one-shot tasks~\cite{Zaidman2010CoEvolutionProductionTest}. For verified programs, this raises a practical question that remains poorly characterized: how brittle does verification become when a developer applies semantics-preserving refactorings to code that was previously verified? Without a benchmarked answer, proof maintenance remains easy to describe informally but hard to study rigorously.

This question is especially important in auto-active verification settings such as Dafny, where source code, specifications, assertions, invariants, and solver-facing proof hints are all part of a single workflow~\cite{Leino2010Dafny}. Dafny reduces proof effort substantially, but successful verification still depends on proof-relevant structure that users learn to shape and maintain, often with the help of recurring verification patterns~\cite{Grov2016VerificationPatternsDafny}. Recent empirical work on Dafny practice also suggests that maintainability and workflow friction remain central to the real cost of formal verification adoption~\cite{Mugnier2025ImpactVerification}. This paper therefore studies proof brittleness not in an interactive theorem prover, but in an SMT-backed auto-active verifier where seemingly mild structural edits can silently perturb obligations that previously discharged automatically.

The broader research landscape makes this gap sharper. Refactoring has been studied extensively in software engineering, while proof refactoring and proof maintenance have been explored mainly in interactive theorem-proving settings~\cite{Whiteside2011ProofScriptRefactoring,Whiteside2013RefactoringProofs,Adams2015Tactician}. At the same time, the Dafny ecosystem has recently attracted growing interest in benchmark construction, testing, and learning-based verification support~\cite{DafnyBench2024,Wang2026TACoDafny,Xu2026DafnyComp}. What is still missing is a reproducible benchmark and empirical characterization of \emph{semantics-preserving refactoring-induced proof breakage in already verified Dafny programs}. P1 targets that missing empirical slice of the space.

Concretely, this paper constructs Dataset~A, a maintenance-centred benchmark of verified Dafny programs paired with transformed versions produced by seven semantics-preserving refactoring classes, and uses it to measure proof brittleness and baseline recovery. The evaluation asks how often verification breaks after refactoring, how those failures cluster by proof-relevant failure mode, and how much of that breakage can be recovered by three simple strategies: re-verification, syntactic transfer, and LLM-only repair. This framing is informed by automated repair and proof-automation literature~\cite{Gazzola2017AutomaticSoftwareRepair,Xia2023APRWithPLM,First2020TacTok,SanchezStern2023Passport,Misu2024AIAssistedDafny}, but the paper is not itself a repair algorithm contribution. Its goal is to quantify the maintenance problem rigorously and to establish the residual repair gap that later structured repair systems must address.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a reproducible Dafny benchmark for refactoring-induced proof brittleness in already verified programs;
  \item an empirical characterization of breakage rates and failure-mode clusters across seven semantics-preserving refactoring classes;
  \item a baseline comparison of re-verification, syntactic transfer, and LLM-only repair that quantifies the residual repair gap while also providing an evaluation substrate for later repair work.
\end{itemize}

The rest of the paper develops this argument in a compact sequence. Section~2 clarifies the conceptual framing needed to separate nearby-version proof brittleness from other forms of change, Section~3 describes Dataset~A and the refactoring benchmark, Section~4 specifies the baselines and evaluation setup, and Section~5 presents the empirical results and their intended interpretation. Section~6 discusses threats to validity, Section~7 positions the paper against related work, and Section~8 concludes by summarizing why the benchmark is a standalone contribution as well as a foundation for later repair work.
